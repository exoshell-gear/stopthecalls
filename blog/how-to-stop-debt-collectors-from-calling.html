How to Stop Debt Collectors from Calling

📅 Updated April 2026 ⏱️ 8 min read

If debt collectors are calling you multiple times a day, contacting you at work, or harassing you with threats — you have legal rights that can make it stop. Under federal law, you can force debt collectors to stop contacting you entirely, and they face serious penalties if they continue.

This guide explains exactly how to stop debt collector calls using the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), including the specific steps to take, what to say (and what not to say), and how to protect yourself if they violate your rights.

Your Legal Rights Under the FDCPA

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act is a federal law that protects consumers from abusive debt collection practices. Here are the most important protections you have:

  • You can tell collectors to stop contacting you — Once you send a written request, they must stop all communication except for two specific reasons (explained below).
  • They cannot call more than 7 times in 7 days — If they call you more frequently, they're violating federal law.
  • No calls before 8 AM or after 9 PM — Calls outside these hours are illegal unless you give permission.
  • They cannot call you at work — If you tell them your employer prohibits personal calls, they must stop calling your workplace.
  • They cannot harass you — Threats, profanity, and repeated calls with intent to annoy are all illegal.
  • They cannot contact your family or friends — Except to locate you, and they cannot discuss your debt with anyone else.

⚠️ Important Distinction

The FDCPA only applies to third-party debt collectors — companies trying to collect debts on behalf of someone else. It does NOT apply to original creditors (like your bank or credit card company) calling about your own account. However, most harassment cases involve third-party collectors, which are covered.

How a Cease and Desist Letter Works

A cease and desist letter is a formal written notice that tells a debt collector to stop all communication with you. Under Section 805(c) of the FDCPA, once a collector receives your written request to stop contact, they are legally required to comply.

What the Letter Does:

  • Stops all phone calls, letters, emails, and text messages from that collector
  • Creates a legal record of your request (critical if they continue calling)
  • Forces them to communicate only through legal action (lawsuit notice) if they choose to continue collection efforts

What It Does NOT Do:

  • It does not erase or eliminate the debt
  • It does not prevent the collector from suing you
  • It does not stop the debt from appearing on your credit report
  • It does not prevent the debt from being sold to another collection agency

A cease and desist letter is a contact-stopping tool, not a debt-elimination tool. But for many people dealing with constant harassment, stopping the calls is the immediate priority.

How to Send a Cease and Desist Letter (Step-by-Step)

If you want to send the letter yourself, here's exactly how to do it correctly:

Step 1: Write the Letter

Your letter should include:

  • Your full name and address
  • The date
  • The debt collector's name and address
  • A clear statement: "I am requesting that you cease all communication with me regarding this debt."
  • Reference to your rights under the FDCPA
  • Your signature

💡 Sample Language

"Pursuant to my rights under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (15 U.S.C. § 1692c), I am requesting that you immediately cease all communication with me regarding the alleged debt referenced as account [number]. This includes phone calls, letters, emails, and text messages. Any further contact will be considered a violation of federal law."

Step 2: Send Via Certified Mail with Return Receipt

This is critical. You need proof that the collector received your letter. Here's why:

  • If they continue calling after receiving your letter, you can sue them for FDCPA violations
  • But you can only sue if you can prove they received it
  • Certified mail with return receipt gives you a signed delivery confirmation

  • Keep a copy of the letter you sent
  • Keep the certified mail receipt
  • Keep the signed return receipt when it comes back
  • Document any calls that occur after the delivery date

What Happens After You Send the Letter

Once the debt collector receives your cease and desist letter, federal law requires them to stop all contact with you. However, there are exactly two exceptions where they can contact you one final time:

  1. To confirm they're stopping collection efforts — They can send you one final notice confirming they will no longer contact you.
  2. To notify you of specific legal action — They can inform you that they are filing a lawsuit or taking other specific legal action against you.

In most cases, calls stop within 5-7 days of the letter being delivered. The collection agency will either:

  • Close the file and return it to the original creditor
  • Sell the debt to another collection agency (who must also honor your cease and desist request if you send them one)
  • File a lawsuit to collect the debt through the court system

⚠️ Critical: Don't Accidentally Restart Communication

If you call the collector or send them a payment after sending a cease and desist letter, you may be waiving your request. Once you send the letter, let it do its job — do not initiate any contact with them.

When Debt Collectors Can Still Contact You

Even after a cease and desist letter, there are a few scenarios where contact may still occur:

If the Debt Is Sold to a New Collector

Your cease and desist request only applies to the specific collection agency you sent it to. If the debt is sold to a different agency, you'll need to send a new letter to that agency. However, many debt buyers will see the original cease and desist in the file and choose not to pursue contact.

If They File a Lawsuit

A cease and desist letter does not prevent a collector from suing you. If they file a lawsuit, you will be served with legal papers (usually by a process server or certified mail). Do not ignore a lawsuit — you must respond to avoid a default judgment.

If You're Contacted by the Original Creditor

Remember, the FDCPA only applies to third-party debt collectors. If the original creditor (the company you originally owed) contacts you directly, the cease and desist letter does not apply to them. However, they are still subject to some consumer protection laws that limit harassment.

What to Do If Collectors Ignore Your Letter

If a debt collector continues to contact you after receiving your cease and desist letter, they are violating federal law. Here's what you can do:

1. Document Every Violation

  • Save all voicemails
  • Log every phone call with date, time, and caller ID
  • Save all letters, emails, and text messages
  • Keep your certified mail receipt and return receipt as proof of delivery

2. File a Complaint

You can report the violation to:

  • Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) — File online at consumerfinance.gov/complaint
  • Federal Trade Commission (FTC) — File at ftc.gov/complaint
  • Your State Attorney General's Office — Most states have consumer protection divisions

3. Sue the Collector

The FDCPA allows you to sue debt collectors who violate the law. If you win, you can recover:

  • Up to $1,000 in statutory damages (even if you suffered no actual financial harm)
  • Actual damages (lost wages, therapy costs, etc.)
  • Attorney's fees and court costs (the collector pays your lawyer, not you)

Many consumer rights attorneys work on contingency, meaning you pay nothing unless you win. If a collector is repeatedly violating your cease and desist letter, consult with a consumer law attorney.
