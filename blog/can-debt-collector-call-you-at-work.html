Can a Debt Collector Call You at Work?

If a debt collector is calling your workplace, you're probably feeling a mix of anger, embarrassment, and fear — fear that your boss might find out, that coworkers might overhear, or that these calls could affect your job. The Law on Workplace Calls: Your FDCPA Rights

Under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), debt collectors cannot call you at work if they know or have reason to know that your employer prohibits such calls. This is federal law, found in 15 U.S.C. § 1692c(a)(3).

Here's what this means in practice:

Initial Contact

A debt collector can call your workplace initially — the first time or two — because they don't yet know your employer's policy. However, once you tell them that your employer prohibits personal calls, or that you're not allowed to receive collection calls at work, they must stop immediately.

The Key Phrase

You don't need written proof of your employer's policy. You don't need to cite the employee handbook. All you need to say is: "My employer does not permit me to receive personal calls at work."

That's it. Once the collector has "reason to know" your employer prohibits these calls — which they do the moment you tell them — any further workplace calls are a violation of federal law.

🚨 They Cannot Demand Proof

Some aggressive collectors will say "We need written documentation from your employer." This is a lie. The FDCPA does not require you to prove your employer's policy. Your verbal statement is sufficient. If they continue calling your workplace after you've told them to stop, they are breaking federal law.

When Workplace Calls Become Illegal

Workplace calls cross the line from annoying to illegal in these situations:

1. After You've Told Them Your Employer Prohibits It

Once you inform the collector (verbally or in writing) that your employer does not allow personal calls, any subsequent calls to your work number are FDCPA violations.

2. If They Ask for You by Name to Coworkers

Debt collectors cannot discuss your debt with third parties. If they call your workplace and tell the receptionist "I'm calling about a debt" or "This is regarding a financial matter," that's a violation. They can only call to locate you, and once they've reached you, they cannot keep calling and speaking to other employees.

3. If They Call Repeatedly

Even before you tell them to stop, repeated calls to your workplace with the intent to harass, embarrass, or pressure you into paying constitute harassment under the FDCPA. The law prohibits "repeated or continuous telephone calls" with intent to annoy.

4. If They Reveal the Debt to Your Employer or Coworkers

Collectors cannot tell your boss, receptionist, or coworkers that you owe a debt. They can only identify themselves and say they're trying to reach you. Any disclosure of debt information to third parties is illegal.

💡 What They CAN Say to Others

When calling your workplace, collectors can only:

  • State their own name
  • Say they are trying to reach you
  • Ask for your phone number or address (to locate you)

They CANNOT mention debt, collections, payment, or anything financial.

How to Stop Workplace Calls Immediately

You have two options to stop debt collectors from calling your workplace:

Option 1: Tell Them Verbally (During the Call)

The fastest way to stop workplace calls is to tell the collector directly during a phone conversation. This works immediately but has one drawback: it's harder to prove later if they violate your request.

When to use this: If you need the calls to stop right now and can't wait for a letter to be delivered.

Option 2: Send Written Notice (Most Legally Protective)

Sending a written notice — via certified mail — creates a permanent legal record that the collector cannot dispute. If they continue calling your workplace after receiving your letter, you have clear evidence of an FDCPA violation that you can use to sue them.

When to use this: When you want the strongest legal protection and proof of your request.

Exactly What to Say to Stop Workplace Calls

If you choose to handle this verbally (during a phone call), here's the exact language to use:

"My employer does not permit me to receive personal calls at work. You are prohibited from calling this number again. You may only contact me in writing at my home address, which is [your address]."

Why This Language Works:

  • "My employer does not permit" — Triggers the FDCPA protection immediately
  • "You are prohibited" — Clear, direct command (not a request)
  • "You may only contact me in writing" — Gives them an alternative so they can't claim they had no way to reach you

What NOT to Say:

❌ "Could you please not call me at work?" (Too weak, sounds like a request)
❌ "It's inconvenient to talk at work." (Not strong enough to trigger FDCPA protection)
❌ "I'll lose my job if you keep calling." (They don't care, and you shouldn't have to explain)

⚠️ Don't Give Them Your Personal Number

You might be tempted to say "Call my cell instead." Don't. Once you provide an alternative number, you're inviting them to keep calling you (just on a different line). If you want all calls to stop, send a full cease and desist letter. If you only want workplace calls to stop, provide your home mailing address for written correspondence only — not a phone number.

If You Want to Send a Written Notice:

Include this paragraph in your cease and desist letter:

"My employer prohibits me from receiving personal calls at work. You are prohibited from contacting me at [workplace name and number]. Any further calls to my workplace will be considered a violation of the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, 15 U.S.C. § 1692c(a)(3). You may contact me only in writing at my home address listed above."

What If Your Employer Finds Out About the Debt?

This is the fear most people have when collectors call their workplace. Here's what you need to know:

Your Employer Cannot Legally Fire You for Having Debt

In most states, it is illegal for an employer to terminate you simply because you owe money to a creditor. However, there are exceptions:

  • If your wages are garnished — Some employers (especially small businesses) may terminate you if your wages are garnished because it creates administrative burden. This is legal in most states.
  • If the debt interferes with job duties — For example, if you work in finance or handle money, and the debt raises concerns about your financial responsibility, that could be grounds for termination in some cases.
  • At-will employment — If you're an at-will employee (most private sector workers), your employer can technically fire you for almost any reason except protected categories (race, religion, etc.). They could use the debt as a pretext.

How to Handle the Conversation

If your boss or HR asks about the calls, you can say:

  • "I'm dealing with a financial matter privately, and I've instructed the company not to contact me here. I apologize for any disruption."
  • "These are collection calls, and I have the legal right to stop them from calling my workplace, which I have done."

You are not required to disclose details about the debt, the amount, or the creditor. Keep it brief and professional.

✅ Most Employers Are Understanding

The reality is that many people deal with debt at some point. Most employers understand this and are more annoyed by the disruption of collection calls than by the fact that you owe money. Stopping the calls quickly is the best way to minimize workplace impact.

What to Do If They Continue Calling Your Workplace

If a debt collector continues to call your work after you've told them to stop (or after they received your written notice), they are violating federal law. Here's what to do:

1. Document Every Call

  • Date and time of each call
  • Name of the person who called (if they gave it)
  • Whether they left a voicemail or spoke to someone
  • What they said (especially if they mentioned the debt to a coworker)

2. Keep Proof of Your Request

If you sent a written letter, keep:

  • A copy of the letter you sent
  • The certified mail receipt
  • The signed return receipt (green card)

If you made the request verbally, write down:

  • The date and time you told them
  • Who you spoke to
  • Exactly what you said

3. File Complaints

Report the violation to:

  • Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) — consumerfinance.gov/complaint
  • Federal Trade Commission (FTC) — ftc.gov/complaint
  • Your State Attorney General — Most states have consumer protection divisions

4. Consider Legal Action

If the collector continues calling your workplace after you've told them to stop, you can sue them under the FDCPA. You can recover:

  • Up to $1,000 in statutory damages (even if you suffered no financial harm)
  • Actual damages (lost wages if you were fired, emotional distress, etc.)
  • Attorney's fees and court costs (paid by the collector, not you)

Many consumer rights attorneys work on contingency, meaning you don't pay unless you win. If a collector has repeatedly violated your workplace contact request, consult with a consumer law attorney.
